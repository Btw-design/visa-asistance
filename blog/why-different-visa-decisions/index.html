Why Do Two People With Similar Profiles Get Different Visa Decisions?

Published: July 10, 2026 12 min read By Ajit Mane Updated 2026

She is a software engineer with five years of experience, earning ₹18 lakhs per year, with a clean passport and a 12-day European tour planned. Her colleague across the hall has the same job title, the same salary, the same travel dates, and the same destination. Yet she received her visa in 12 days, while he was handed a refusal letter under Article 32(1)(b) of the Schengen Visa Code.

This scenario plays out every day in visa applications across the world. Two applicants with near-identical profiles approach the same embassy with the same purpose of travel — and walk away with completely different outcomes. It is confusing, frustrating, and often feels arbitrary. But is it really random? In this guide, we share practical visa application tips to help you understand and navigate these differences.

The short answer is no. Visa decisions are not random. They are the result of a systematic evaluation by consular officers trained to assess risk, intent, and credibility. Many applicants ask "why visa gets rejected" when their equally qualified friend was approved — the answer lies in subtle but critical differences that determine the outcome.

This comprehensive guide explores the hidden factors that cause different visa decisions for similar-looking profiles — and what you can do to ensure your application lands on the right side of the decision. Understanding common visa rejection reasons and avoiding visa application mistakes is the first step toward getting approved.

Key Insight: The "Similar Profile" Myth

On the surface, two applicants may appear identical: same profession, same income bracket, same destination, same travel dates. But consular officers evaluate over 40 distinct data points in every application. A difference in just two or three of these points can shift a decision from approval to rejection. This article reveals exactly which points matter most.

The Fallacy of "Similar Profiles"

When an applicant says "my friend with the same profile got the visa," they are often overlooking significant differences in how their applications were prepared and presented. Here is the truth: no two visa applications are truly identical, even when the applicants share the same demographic characteristics.

Consular officers across countries evaluate visa applications based on a standard risk assessment framework that considers:

  • Financial credibility — Not just how much money an applicant has, but how they acquired it and whether it reflects genuine financial stability
  • Immigration risk — The likelihood that an applicant will overstay or violate visa conditions
  • Purpose credibility — Whether the stated reason for travel is supported by convincing evidence
  • Compliance history — Past visa compliance, previous rejections, and travel behaviour
  • Home country ties — The strength of an applicant's social, economic, and professional connections to their home country

Even when two applicants appear similar in income and occupation, each of these factors can be supported by vastly different documentation, leading to different outcomes.

10 Hidden Factors That Cause Different Visa Decisions

Let us examine the specific factors that consular officers weigh — factors that can make two similar profiles produce completely different results.

1. Quality vs Quantity of Financial Documents

This is perhaps the single most common hidden factor. Two applicants with the same bank balance can receive different decisions based on how that balance was accumulated and presented.

Factor Applicant A (Approved) Applicant B (Rejected)
Bank Balance ₹5.2 lakhs with salary credits over 6 months ₹4.8 lakhs deposited as one lump sum 2 weeks before application
Statement Pattern Regular salary credits every month, gradual spending Irregular deposits, large unexplained withdrawals
Income Proof 6 months salary slips matching bank credits Salary slips present but amounts do not match bank entries
Additional Assets Fixed deposits, mutual fund statements, property documents No additional asset documentation

Consular officers are trained to recognise financial patterns. A large lump-sum deposit shortly before the application date is a red flag — it suggests the applicant borrowed money purely for the visa rather than demonstrating genuine financial capacity. On the other hand, a consistent salary credit pattern over 6 months shows stable income and genuine financial health.

Pro Tip: Never make a large lump-sum deposit just for visa purposes. Instead, maintain a healthy balance consistently for at least 3–6 months before applying. For Schengen visa bank balance guidance, see our Schengen Visa Bank Balance Guide.

2. Employment Stability & Income Consistency

Two applicants may both be "software engineers earning ₹18 lakhs," but one has been in the same job for 5 years while the other switched jobs 3 months ago. To a consular officer, these represent very different risk profiles.

Employment factors that matter:

  • Job tenure — Longer tenure signals stability and stronger ties to the home country
  • Company reputation — Employment at a well-known MNC or established firm adds credibility
  • Leave approval letter — A proper letter with specific dates, sanctioning annual leave, confirms the employer expects you back
  • Business ownership — Self-employed applicants need additional documents like business registration, GST returns, and IT returns

For a detailed checklist of documents required for various employment types, see our Visa Document Checklist 2026.

3. Travel History & Previous Visa Compliance

Travel history is one of the most powerful factors in visa assessments. It is also where similar profiles often diverge most significantly.

1

First-time Traveller vs Experienced Traveller

An applicant who has visited countries like UAE, Thailand, Singapore, or Malaysia and returned on time has already demonstrated compliance with visa conditions. A first-time international traveller has no such track record, making the assessment inherently riskier for the consular officer.

2

Schengen/US/UK/Australia Travel History

Previous travel to high-standard countries (Schengen states, USA, UK, Canada, Australia, Japan) carries even more weight. It proves you have already been vetted by a stringent immigration system and complied with its rules.

3

Visa Stamps vs Visa Stickers

Past visa stamps on your passport provide concrete proof of travel history. If your previous passport (with those stamps) is lost or expired, attach a copy of the old passport if available, or mention it in your cover letter.

4. Clarity of Purpose of Visit

"Tourism" is a broad description. A vague itinerary can undermine an otherwise strong application. Consular officers ask: Does this applicant genuinely know what they plan to do in my country? Does their itinerary make sense?

What makes a strong purpose statement:

  • Day-by-day itinerary with specific attractions, restaurants, or activities planned
  • Hotel bookings that match the itinerary (not just refundable cancellations)
  • Internal flights or train bookings within the destination country
  • A cover letter that tells a coherent story about why this trip matters to the applicant

What makes a weak purpose statement:

  • Generic phrases like "sightseeing" or "tourism" with no details
  • Hotel bookings from booking.com that are clearly cancellable with no genuine reservation
  • An itinerary that does not account for travel time between cities
  • A cover letter that reads like a template downloaded from the internet

5. Strength of Ties to Home Country

This is the cornerstone of visa risk assessment. The question every consular officer seeks to answer is: Will this applicant return to their home country?

Strong ties include:

  • Permanent employment that expects your return
  • Immediate family residing in the home country (spouse, children, elderly parents)
  • Property ownership (home, land, vehicle)
  • Ongoing education or professional commitments
  • Business ownership or partnership in local ventures

Weak ties include:

  • Unemployment or unstable job history
  • No immediate family obligations
  • Rental accommodation with no property assets
  • Recently quit job or resigned from a position
Critical Warning: Never fabricate home ties. Providing false documents (fake employment letters, forged property documents, fabricated bank statements) is immigration fraud. If discovered, it can result in a multi-year ban from applying for visas to that country, and in severe cases, a permanent inadmissibility.

6. Interview Performance

For countries that require a visa interview (USA, UK, Canada, Schengen in some cases), the interview can make or break an application. Two applicants with identical paper dossiers can receive different outcomes based purely on how they present themselves at the interview counter.

Interview factors that matter:

Factor Strong Applicant Weak Applicant
Confidence Answers clearly, maintains eye contact, does not fidget Hesitates, gives vague answers, looks nervous
Consistency Answers match documents exactly Contradicts own application form or cover letter
Knowledge Knows their itinerary, hotel names, and purpose clearly Cannot name hotels or explain why they chose this destination
English Proficiency Can communicate without an interpreter Relies heavily on interpreter or struggles with basic questions
Return Intent Confidently mentions job, family, or studies they are returning to Vague about post-trip plans or says "I will see"

7. Consular Officer Discretion (The Human Element)

This is the factor that feels most arbitrary, yet it follows a structured logic. Consular officers are experienced professionals trained to assess applications consistently. However, like all human decision-makers, they bring individual judgment, experience, and intuition to each case.

What influences officer discretion:

  • Previous case experience — An officer who recently handled several fraudulent applications from a particular region or profession may scrutinise similar applications more closely
  • Current immigration trends — If an embassy has seen a spike in overstays from a specific demographic, officers may apply stricter scrutiny
  • Document presentation — A well-organised, neatly presented application with all documents properly arranged creates a positive impression
  • The "gut feeling" — Based on years of experience, officers develop an intuition for identifying applications that do not feel genuine

While the human element exists, it is important to understand that officers follow standard operating procedures and visa codes. Discretion operates within boundaries, not outside them.

8. Application Timing & Seasonal Factors

The timing of an application can influence outcomes in subtle but measurable ways.

1

Peak Season vs Off-Season

During peak travel months (May–September for Schengen, November–January for Australia/NZ), embassies process higher volumes. While this does not change the criteria for approval, it can lead to longer processing times and, in some cases, more conservative decisions due to the volume pressure.

2

Application Timing Within Your Work History

A person applying immediately after starting a new job is riskier than someone who has been in the same role for years. Similarly, applying shortly after a large loan disbursement or while on notice period raises additional questions.

3

Application Submission Window

For Schengen visas, applying up to 6 months before travel is allowed. Applying exactly 6 months before (as soon as the window opens) versus 3 weeks before travel sends different signals about the applicant's planning and genuine intent.

9. Country-Specific Risk Assessment

Different countries have different risk profiles for different nationalities and regions. The same individual may be approved for a Thailand visa but rejected for a UK visa — not because the person changed, but because each country's immigration authority applies its own risk assessment.

For Indian applicants, the following general patterns have been observed in recent years:

Destination Typical Approval Rate (Indian Applicants) Key Assessment Focus
Schengen Area 75–85% Financial credibility, travel history, purpose clarity
USA 70–80% Interview performance, home ties, previous travel
UK 80–88% Financial documents, purpose, compliance history
Canada 75–85% English proficiency, home ties, immigration intent
Australia 80–90% Employment stability, financial capacity, health insurance
Thailand/UAE 90–95% Basic financial proof, return ticket

This explains why two people with similar profiles may succeed for some destinations but not others. For detailed processing times across different destinations, see our UK Visa Processing Times and Schengen Visa Complete Guide.

10. Document Presentation & Completeness

This is the most preventable factor. Two applications with identical information can receive different outcomes if one is incomplete, disorganised, or contains errors.

Document Checklist for a Strong Application:
  • All required documents from the official embassy checklist present
  • Documents arranged in the order specified by the embassy or VAC
  • Photocopies of all original documents (originals for verification at VAC)
  • Self-attested copies where required
  • Bank statements stamped by the bank (not just online printouts)
  • Cover letter that is personalised, not a template
  • Travel insurance meeting the minimum coverage requirements
  • Correct visa category applied for (tourist, business, transit, etc.)
  • Passport with sufficient validity (typically 6 months + 2 blank pages)
  • Photographs meeting the exact size and background specifications
Common Visa Application Mistakes That Cause Rejection: Missing signatures on the application form, incorrect visa fee payment, passport expiring within 3 months of return date, photographs with the wrong background colour, incomplete travel insurance (missing Schengen-wide coverage), and submitting the application at the wrong jurisdiction/VAC. Any one of these can cause a rejection even for a perfectly qualified applicant.

How Consular Officers Evaluate Applications

Understanding the evaluation framework helps demystify why similar profiles get different decisions. Most embassies use a structured assessment process that considers three core questions:

1

Is the Purpose Genuine?

The officer evaluates whether the stated purpose of travel (tourism, business, medical treatment, study) matches the visa category applied for and whether the supporting documents convincingly support this purpose. A tourist visa application with no hotel bookings, no itinerary, and a vague cover letter fails this test.

2

Can the Applicant Support Themselves?

The officer checks whether the applicant has sufficient funds to cover the entire trip — including accommodation, food, local transport, emergencies, and return travel. The required amount varies by country and duration, but the key is demonstrating how these funds were obtained, not just the final balance.

3

Will the Applicant Return?

This is the ultimate question. The officer weighs all evidence of home ties — employment, family, property, ongoing commitments — against any risk factors like weak financials, no travel history, or socio-economic conditions that might encourage overstaying. The decision is a risk calculation, not a certainty.

Real-World Case Studies

Case Study 1: The Same-Office Difference

Applicants: Two marketing managers at the same IT company, both earning ₹15 lakhs annually, both applying for Schengen tourism to Switzerland for 10 days.

Outcome: Applicant A approved, Applicant B rejected.

Why? Applicant A had been with the company for 4 years, provided 6 months of salary-slip-aligned bank statements with a ₹4 lakh consistent balance, a detailed day-by-day Switzerland itinerary, previous travel to Dubai and Thailand, and a cover letter explaining their 10th wedding anniversary celebration plan. Applicant B had joined the company 4 months ago, submitted bank statements with a ₹3 lakh lump-sum deposit made 10 days before the application, a generic "sightseeing" cover letter, no travel history, and no hotel bookings beyond the first night.

Case Study 2: The Bank Balance Trap

Applicants: Two business owners from the same city applying for US B1/B2 visas. Both have ITRs showing ₹25 lakhs annual income. Both have ₹8 lakhs in their bank accounts.

Outcome: Applicant A approved, Applicant B rejected.

Why? Applicant A's ₹8 lakhs balance had been maintained consistently for 8 months with regular business deposits and GST payments visible in the statement. Applicant B's ₹8 lakhs appeared as a single cash deposit 5 days before the application, and the officer noted that their ITR showed income of ₹25 lakhs but the bank statement showed almost no regular business transactions. The application was rejected for "insufficient evidence of financial means — source of funds not established."

How to Maximise Your Visa Approval Chances

The question "why visa gets rejected" often comes down to a few preventable issues. Based on the factors above, here is a practical action plan to maximise your visa approval chances:

Action Plan — How to Get Visa Approved:
  • Plan ahead — start document preparation at least 3–4 months before your intended travel date
  • Maintain a healthy bank balance with consistent salary/business credits for at least 3–6 months
  • Avoid lump-sum deposits shortly before the application — let your money "age" in the account
  • Build travel history gradually — start with easier destinations (Thailand, UAE, Singapore) before applying to stringent countries (Schengen, USA, UK)
  • Prepare a detailed, genuine itinerary with confirmed (or at least refundable) bookings
  • Write a personal, honest cover letter — do not use generic templates
  • Ensure all documents are complete, self-attested, and arranged in order
  • Attach additional assets — fixed deposits, mutual funds, property documents, shares
  • If rejected, understand the exact reason before reapplying — do not simply re-submit the same application
  • Consider consulting a professional visa agency for complex cases or after a rejection
Pro Tip: Document everything in writing. A well-prepared cover letter that proactively addresses potential concerns (why you chose this destination, what ties you have to India, how you will fund the trip, your post-trip plans) can significantly influence the consular officer's perception of your application.
